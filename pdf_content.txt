CHAPTER 1 - INTRODUCTION 
 
1.1 Project Overview 
The Philippines ranks as one of the globe’s most hazard-exposed nations hit often by storms, 
flooding,  quakes,  slope  failures,  along  with  volcanic  activity  (World  Risk  Report,  2023).  For  
each   threat   type,   a   separate   agency   handles   surveillance   and   alerts   like   PAGASA, 
PHIVOLCS,  or  NDRRMC;  yet  they  operate  in  isolation  when  sharing  updates  via  online  
platforms,  social  networks,  text  alerts,  plus  press  statements  (PAGASA,  2023;  PHIVOLCS,  
2023; NDRRMC, 2023). Because coordination lacks integration, public response may suffer 
from mixed signals, slow comprehension of danger levels, uneven readiness across regions. 
This study introduces a mobile app called "Babala sa Sakuna" developed for disaster alerts 
in the Philippines. Rather than combining multiple tools, it works as one centralized system 
focused  on  smartphones.  Information  comes  from  reliable  agencies,  then  gets  filtered  into  
simple  updates  anyone  can  understand.  Instead  of  complex  layouts,  warnings  appear  in  
straightforward  visuals  and  messages.  Besides  alerts,  users  also  gain  access  to  readiness  
guides  tailored  to  local  risks.  To  support  real-time  awareness,  residents  may  submit  
observations directly through the interface. 
 
1.2 Motivation and Problem Statement 
In rapidly changing crises like when a powerful typhoon hits or sudden flooding begins, people 
usually depend on both government alerts and unchecked posts from social networks. Studies 
in  the  Philippines  show  that  scattered  updates  mixed  with  false  rumors  may  delay  proper  
reactions (Bernardo, 2019; Flores &amp; Abalos, 2021). Often, individuals find it hard to know 
which alerts are up to date, where impacts are happening, or what specific steps to follow. 
The core issue tackled here is the absence of a single, easy-to-use mobile app tailored to local 
needs,  which  brings  together  disaster-related  data  for  people  in  the  Philippines.  Instead  of  
depending  on  fragmented  updates  and  unofficial  sources,  the  solution  introduces  a  unified  
platform where individuals can check real-time warnings, explore danger zones via maps, and 
get actionable safety tips, even without internet. 
 
1.3 Project Template and Scope 
This project applies Template 10.1 in the CM3050 Mobile Development module to build a 
mobile app aimed at improving community preparedness for emergencies, it targets real-
time response during local disasters. While designed for practical use, the system supports 
users before, during, and after crisis events. The approach combines simple navigation with 
key alert features, helping individuals act quickly when needed the most. 
For now, the focus stays on building a working front-end model with fake data this means 
made-up alerts and test danger zones. Instead of full features, it allows simple movement 
between main pages like the overview screen, warning info, risk map, task list, and report 
section. Besides that, there’s a part designed to work without internet, giving critical help 

when signals are weak or missing. Alongside, early tests show how the system acts and 
whether users find it manageable. 
Integration with live government alerts isn't part of this version; instead, the setup allows 
room for it down the line. Although direct data sharing now isn’t active, the framework 
supports linking to public alert sources later on - when expanded in future updates.
 
1.4 Aim and Objectives 
The  main  goal  is  designing  a  mobile  app  prototype tool that focuses  on  multiple  hazards,  
helping Filipino groups grasp risks and act accordingly when disasters threaten. 
To reach this goal, the project sets these aims: 
• To examine current disaster information platforms while spotting weaknesses in reach, 
ease of use, and regional adaptation. 
• To  define  operational  plus  technical  needs  for  a  mobile  app  warning  about  multiple  
hazards. 
• To build a system layout along with an interface enabling instant warnings, risk zone 
maps, checklists usable without internet, also allowing local updates. 
• To build a test version showing key features especially the warning panel plus access 
to risk details. 
• To assess the feature prototype through a suitable method focused on users.

CHAPTER 2 - LITERATURE REVIEW 
 
2.1 Disaster Risk and Communication in the Philippines 
The  Philippines  often  ranks  high  in  global  disaster  risk  because  it  faces  frequent  natural  
hazards  along  with  social  and  economic  weaknesses  (World  Risk  Report,  2023;  UNDRR,  
2022).  Over  twenty  typhoons  move  into  the  nation’s  monitoring  zone  annually  (PAGASA,  
2023); meanwhile, its location on the Pacific Ring of Fire increases threats from earthquakes 
and  eruptions  (PHIVOLCS,  2023).  Because  of  this,  clear  and  fast  communication  during 
emergencies becomes essential for public safety. 
Research on disaster messaging in the Philippines points to challenges like dependence on 
conventional  outlets,  inconsistent  digital  access,  alongside  widespread  unchecked  content  
online (Bernardo, 2019; Flores &amp; Abalos, 2021). Despite rising internet and phone usage 
(Santos, 2018), incorporating tech into structured alert networks is still limited. 
 
2.2 Existing Official Information Sources 
Multiple groups in the Philippines currently share info on disasters 
• PAGASA  handles  weather  predictions,  storm  updates,  or  flood  alerts  (PAGASA,  
2023). 
• PHIVOLCS releases alerts about quakes, volcanic activity, or tidal waves (PHIVOLCS, 
2023). 
• NDRRMC manages nationwide emergency actions; it also shares verified updates and 
status summaries (NDRRMC, 2027). 
Every organization usually runs its own site while sharing alerts through Facebook updates, 
PDF  files,  or  media  statements.  Because  these  sources  are  split  up,  people  need to check 
several platforms at once to understand the full danger, something that can be hard or even 
impossible when a crisis unfolds quickly. 
 
2.3 Global Platforms and Tools 
Beyond  government  bodies,  worldwide  platforms  like  Google  SOS  Alerts  deliver  situation 
specific updates when major emergencies occur (Google, 2023). Such alerts appear through 
Search,  Maps,  or  phone  notifications,  giving  people  general  insights  into  nearby  risks.  Still,  
these global systems usually cover wide areas instead of precise local zones like individual 
barangays and often function separately from official national alert networks. 
An  additional  key  case  is  Project  NOAH,  initially  launched  by  the  Philippine  state  to  merge  
various data types like rain measurements, river heights, or flood predictions into an interactive 
map system (Project NOAH, 2020). Despite its progress in displaying risk details more clearly, 
when authorities stopped funding it, this weakened the tool’s long-term use alongside present 
emergency procedures. 
 

2.4 Community-Based and Academic Approaches 
Studies into local disaster alert networks highlight the value of grassroots insights alongside 
public  involvement.  Flores  and  Abalos  (2021)  show  that  hazard  maps  made  by  residents,  
combined  with  area-specific updates,  may  strengthen  state-issued  alerts  particularly  in  
isolated  or  under-resourced  regions.  Still,  blending  citizen  inputs  with  government  records  
needs thoughtful planning so misinformation isn't accidentally shared. 
Bernardo  (2019)  points  out  difficulties  in  sharing  risk  details  with  diverse  groups,  since  
confusing  terms  and  uneven  messaging  styles  can  lead  to  errors.  As  a  result,  fresh  tools  
should focus on clear, straightforward, uniform design particularly within smartphone apps. 
 
2.5 Mobile Technologies and Usability Considerations 
Mobile  phones  are  now  the  main  way  people  get  info  in  the  Philippines,  especially  using  
smartphones  (Santos,  2018).  Because  of  this  shift,  apps  can  help  share  disaster  alerts  yet 
they  come  with  usability  hurdles.  Under  high  pressure,  users  may  struggle  to  focus;  
meanwhile, poor signal or low power adds difficulty. Moreover, designs need to work for those 
who read less well or lack tech experience. 
Human-computer  interaction  studies  show  that  simple  layouts,  easy-to-follow  menus,  or  
reduced  mental  effort  matter  most  in  crisis  situations.  Tools  like  step-by-step  lists,  symbol-
driven  interfaces,  yet  location-based  displays  may  speed  up  threat  recognition provided 
they’re  built  thoughtfully.  Functionality  without  internet  access  is  likewise  advised  when  
connectivity fails amid emergencies. 
 
2.6 Critical Evaluation of Existing Systems 
The current systems mentioned earlier show notable advantages they’re credible, frequently 
include detailed data, while also advancing gradually due to refined models and tracking 
methods (PAGESA, 2023; PHIVOLCS, 2023; NDRRMC, 2023; UNDRR, 2022). Still, various 
ongoing weaknesses matter for this study. 
• Disjointed data sources split details across platforms, so people must piece together 
insights themselves yet no unified view exists to simplify understanding because 
systems operate in isolation rather than sync efforts. 
• Some global tools lack precise location data local info like neighbourhoods might be 
missing. National platforms often have layouts hard to understand for average 
people. 
• Usability problems arise because numerous government sites lack mobile 
optimization; meanwhile, PDF updates prove hard to navigate on smartphones. 
• Lack of offline functionality: many platforms depend on internet access even though 
connections often fail when crises occur. 
• Poor inclusion of local views: formal setups rarely gather organised feedback from 
impacted individuals. 
Such  challenges  highlight  the  need  for  a  single  app  focused  on  ease  of  use,  broad hazard 
alerts, plus location-specific info. This initiative takes advantage of current platforms especially 

their trusted sources but improves them by prioritising mobile access, real-user needs, offline 
tools, along with simple ways for communities to share updates. 
 
2.7 Summary of Literature Gaps 
In short, existing studies along with real world applications suggest: 
• The Philippines has solid systems in place to track natural hazards. 
• Interaction and how users feel about it stand out as less effective links in the process. 
• App-based tools can work due to widespread mobile use though thoughtful planning 
and testing matter. 
The suggested project addresses these shortcomings through attention to interface layout, 
combining data sources, readiness tools without internet access using assessment methods 
based on human-computer interaction ideas. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 

CHAPTER 3 - DESIGN 
3.1 Design Goals 
From  prior  research  plus  an  examination  of  existing  issues,  the  development  of  the  alert  
system follows these objectives: 
• Crisis situations need simple messages, using common words helps prevent 
misunderstandings, because complicated phrasing increases mistakes, according to 
Bernardo (2019). 
• Fast understanding helps individuals recognize what’s happening along with required 
steps quickly, fitting human to  machine communication standards during 
emergencies. 
• Emphasize local conditions by pointing out specific locations and affected areas, 
tackling gaps in detailed barangay level information noted in earlier research. 
• Resilience: Provide key guidance even when signals are weak, Santos (2018) found 
that numerous Filipinos struggle with connectivity during crises. 
• Adaptability: Allows future connections to real-time data sources via a scalable 
structure, using modular components that support system integration when needed.
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 

 
3.2 System Architecture (High Level) 
The setup is thought of as a flexible phone app featuring these main parts:

Key Architecture Decisions:

The structure includes five key levels: 
1. Presentation Layer (Mobile UI) 
 
Builds all screen users see like dashboards, alerts, maps, checklists, also reports. 
Primarily built for Android, though adaptable to other systems using React Native. 
 
2. Application Logic Layer 
 
Handles  navigation  alongside  app  state like  active  alerts  or  filter  options  while 
managing  operations  including  sorting  warnings  based  on  category  or  seriousness 
using  conditional  checks  instead  of  direct  commands.  Checks  simple  rules  for  
community submissions, like missing entries. 
 
3. Data Layer (Prototype) 
 
In  this  version,  the  system  relies  on  fixed  JSON  files  kept  offline  to  show  warnings,  
danger areas, and task lists though these are not pulled from external sources. Instead, 
they’re  embedded  directly  into  the  interface  for  quick  access  while  limiting  dynamic  
updates during use. In future versions, this module could link to online data sources 
from  PAGASA,  also  pulling  inputs  from  PHIVOLCS,  while  integrating  feeds  from  
NDRRMC. 
 
4. Mapping Service Integration 
 
Uses  Google  Maps  SDK  to  show  danger  areas;  also  displays  where  the  user  is  
(Google,  2023).  In  the  early  model  stage,  risky  areas  appear  through  added  visual  
tags. 
 
5. Local Storage / Offline Support 
Keeps  the  emergency  checklist  along  with  saved  warnings  directly  on  the  phone  
through Async Storage. Keeps key advice available without internet connection.

3.3 Functional Design 
The main functions of the system show up in its primary components: 
1.   Alert Module 
• Shows active warnings including kind, seriousness, brief description, also when last 
changed. 
• Let’s users filter based on danger type such as storm, flooding, quake, or eruption. 
• Let’s users access specific details about every alert through direct links. 
 
2.   Hazard Map Module 
• Shows risky zones using colored layers.  Red means severe, orange stands for high, 
amber indicates medium, while blue shows minor risks. 
• Shows roughly where the user is, if allowed by settings. 
• Links to certain warnings, showing impacted areas using circular zones. 
3.   Checklist Module 
• Offers  organized  emergency  lists  with  39  practical  steps,  designed  for  use  without  
internet access. 
• Split  into  three  stages:  "Before",  followed  by  "During",  then  ending  with  "After"  an  
occurrence. 
• Enables fast checking of suggested steps by their importance order. 
 
4.   Reporting Module 
• Users can send basic reports about local issues by choosing a category like flooded 
areas or obstructed streets and including a brief note. 
• In the first model, data files stay on local devices. 
• In  future  updates,  reports  might  go  to  a  central  server  where local  authorities  would  
then check and combine them using secure channels. 
5.   Navigation Framework 
• Enables navigation from dashboard to map, then checklist; continues to report before 
reaching detailed screens. 
• Uses a bottom bar to switch between key screens, while moving deeper into sections 
through sequential steps. 
 
3.4 User Flow 
The primary path users follow aims to stay straightforward, meeting targets around 
quickness plus clear communication: 
1. App starts the screen shows current warnings ordered by how serious they are. 
2. User checks the list, then picks an alert. 
3. The Alert details window displays impacted zones along with when they occurred, 
followed by suggested steps to take. 
4. After that, users may: 
a.   access the Hazard Map to see where risks are located, also how severe they 
might be, 
b.   use the Checklist to find what steps to take next. 
5. The user may change tabs at any time through the lower navigation panel. 

This sequence matches the main concerns people usually face in emergencies what’s going 
on, where it is occurring, how it impacts them, and what actions to take.

3.8 DEI and Accessibility Considerations 
Diversity,  equity,  and  inclusion  shape  the  system’s  structure helping  people  regardless  of  
reading  ability,  age,  or  internet  quality.  High-contrast  visuals  along  with  straightforward  
warning cues cut confusion during urgent moments. Alerts appear in simple terms, avoiding 
technical  jargon.  Critical  steps  and  safety  advice  work  without  Wi-Fi,  targeting  areas  where  
mobile  networks  fail  often.  Future  updates  will  offer  content  in  multiple  languages,  such  as  
English  plus  Tagalog,  increasing  reach  across  linguistic  groups.  Button  sizes  and  layout  
spacing accommodate small displays and those with limited hand mobility. Together, these 
decisions target gaps tied to hardware limits, mental effort, and spoken language differences.

4.1 Prototype Purpose and Scope 
The initial version highlights how users interact with the emergency alert system, specifically:

4.2 Implementation Overview 
The prototype used React Native alongside Expo SDK 54, matching the plans from Chapter 
3. It includes: 
• A bottom-tab navigator with routes for Dashboard, Map, Checklist and Report 
• A  stack-based  system  moves  users  from  the  main  screen  to  specific  alerts  using 
transitions that maintain context flow while enabling return paths through hierarchical 
ordering 
• Reusable parts for filter chips, alert cards also severity indicators 
• Structure handling data tasks using services like AlertService or CacheService 
• Combining  expo-location  to  handle  user  location  tracking  through  device  sensors  
instead of manual input 
• Storing data locally when offline using AsyncStorage 
 
Because alert data uses TypeScript objects that are strictly typed, every alert includes an ID, 
a title, plus details like description. Each one specifies its category such as typhoon or flood 
or even earthquake and volcano. Severity ranges from critical down to low, influencing how 
the system displays them. Affected regions are listed alongside time-related info along with 
suggested actions. On the dashboard, alerts get sorted based on seriousness instead of just 
kind. Filters  help  narrow  results  either  by  risk  level  or  event  type.  Once  someone  picks  an  
alert,  its  unique  ID  moves  forward  through  Expo  Router’s  parameter  setup.  That  key  then  
loads full content inside the detail view. 
 
The hazard map display combines Google Maps SDK along with markers showing warning 
spots, shaded according to risk level. Around each threat, circular zones illustrate the impacted 
region. At the base, a sliding panel presents specifics of the chosen alert, including route tools. 
 
The  checklist  interface  includes  39  entries  divided  across  three  stages  (Before:  12,  During:  
13, After: 14). Data is saved on-device so it works without internet. On the report page, users 
fill  out  a  verified  template  choosing  a  category,  adding  a  place  if  needed,  along  with  a  
mandatory explanation.

4.4 Evaluation Approach 
This  project  emphasizes  how  users  interact  with  the  system,  not  just  technical  efficiency;  
therefore,  assessment  draws  from  human-computer  interaction  techniques.  Instead  of  
measuring speed or accuracy alone, it uses practical observation and feedback sessions 
 
Heuristic Evaluation 
The layout was checked using Nielsen’s 10 usability rules; for instance, system feedback like 
loading signs or severity tags improves clarity. Regarding alignment with everyday experience, 
known hazard symbols were used instead of abstract ones. Users can navigate back or switch 
tabs  easily  this  supports  autonomy  during  tasks.  Colour  use  stays  uniform  across  screens,  
which  aids  recognition over  time.  To  reduce  mistakes,  forms  include  live  validation  before  
submission. 
Task-Based User Testing (Small-Scale) 
A  handful  of  selected  participants  local  Filipino  students  and  relatives  aware  of  regional  
disaster situations were invited to carry out defined activities 
1. Find the worst active warning 
2. Go to the hazard map then explain where danger might occur 
3. Find guidance that explains the steps to take 
4. Check the list to identify actions needed prior to a disaster 
5. Send in an easy update from the local area 
 
During these sessions, the observer noted where participants hesitated, misunderstood or 
struggled with navigation. 
Post-Session Feedback 
Respondents answered broad questions on aspects they saw as understandable, unclear, 
comforting, or absent. 
4.5 Initial Evaluation and Findings 
First tests involving few participants (n=5) revealed these results: 
Strengths: 
1. The dashboard-style interface, along with filter chips, was called “simple to grasp” by 
4 out of 5 users. 
2. Participants liked having severity indicators along with time stamps displayed on alert 
cards. 
3. The colour-based alert levels became clear quickly through short use. 
4. The combination of notifications, maps, or checklists within one application felt helpful 
also calming to users 
5. Offline checklists mattered a lot particularly for those hit by network problems in earlier 
storms. 

6. Task  completion  stood  at  a  strong  level;  four  out  of  five  participants  finished  all  
assignments independently 
 
Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement: 
1. Two out of five participants were unsure at first about what the colour-coded severity 
labels  meant;  therefore,  providing  a  brief  guide  during  initial  access  could  help.  
Because  clarity  improved  after  exposure,  early  guidance  may  support  quicker  
understanding. Since no one figured it instantly, integrating a simple explanation upon 
entry   might   enhance   usability.   While   most   caught   on   over   time,   immediate   
comprehension would likely rise with contextual cues present from the start. 
2. The map needed an active Google Maps API key to work properly. When tested using 
a dummy version, people found it provided fewer details than anticipated. 
3. The  reporting  function’s  goal  wasn’t  clearly  explained.  Details  like  recipient  roles  or  
post-submission steps were missing. 
4. A single user proposed bigger tap areas for filter chips when using compact displays. 
Results  match  earlier  work  highlighting  precise  messaging  about  risks  especially  reducing  
uncertainty (Bernardo, 2019); similar outcomes appear in studies by Flores and Abalos (2021). 
 
4.6 Planned Improvements 
Given the first review alongside intended goals, upcoming versions will include these 
upgrades: 
 
1.   Improved map links 
Full use of Google Maps API featuring live updates on one risk kind like quake details 
from PHIVOLCS. 
2.   Severity Guide and Details 
Include  a  startup  screen  or  pop-up  guide  showing  what  each  level  means,  using  
colours  and  symbols  helpful  for  new  users.  While  launching  the  app,  display  brief  
explanations through tooltips or an intro panel to clarify ratings. Instead of assuming 
familiarity,  present  clear  examples  matching  icons  to  their  meaning  early  on.  This  
supports understanding without relying on prior knowledge. 
3.   Enhanced reporting clarity 
Show a short note on what happens to community feedback, while including progress 
updates for each submission. 
4.   Enhanced visual layout 
Enlarge tap areas for filter tags while adjusting gaps this boosts usability on compact 
screens.  Layout  changes  help  users  interact  more  easily,  especially  where  space  is  
limited. Bigger buttons plus clearer separation make navigation simpler for everyone. 
5.   Use Expo Notifications to send key alerts if the app isn't open. These messages pop 
up instantly, even when users aren't actively using it. 
6.   Multilingual Option 
Include  Tagalog  to  reach  more  users  this  improves  access  across  different  groups  
while expanding usability naturally. 
 
4.7 Summary 
The feature prototype shows the main idea single app for disaster warnings and local alerts in 
the Philippines can work in real-world conditions while meeting basic usability standards. Early 
testing  with  five  participants  indicates  people  grasp  how  alerts,  maps,  and  checklists  fit  
together most completed tasks successfully (80%) without help. However, some parts need 

improvement: users struggled at first with the color-coded labels, also the intent behind the 
report function wasn't fully clear. 
 
Later studies will aim to improve live data handling in the prototype, finish integrating Google 
Maps, while also testing the system more thoroughly using a bigger group of users.
